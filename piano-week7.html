What this week is about

The dominant 7th chord is the most emotionally powerful chord in all of music. It creates an urgent, unresolved tension that screams to resolve to the 1 chord. B♭ Major adds a second flat (E♭), and this week’s song lives right at home in it: “What a Wonderful World” by Louis Armstrong — B♭ Major, chords 1–4–5 (B♭–E♭–F), slow tempo, one of the most beloved melodies ever written. You also perfect the 12-bar blues with all three dominant 7th chords, and learn rubato — the technique that makes music breathe.

🎺

B♭ Major

Two flats: B♭ and E♭. The home key of jazz trumpet, R&B, and gospel. Louis Armstrong lived here. A rich, warm tonality with enormous repertoire.

⚡

Dominant 7th

Add a flat 7th to any major chord: B♭7 = B♭–D–F–A♭. Creates urgent tension that resolves powerfully to Chord 1. The engine of the blues and jazz.

🎵

12-Bar Blues Upgrade

All three chords become dominant 7ths: B♭7, E♭7, F7. Continuous buzzing tension throughout. This is the complete blues form used in every blues, rock, and jazz recording.

🌊

Rubato

Intentional flexibility of tempo. Slowing slightly before a resolution, then settling back. The technique that makes a performance feel human rather than mechanical.

Build the B♭ Major Scale

B♭–C–D–E♭–F–G–A–B♭. Two flats: B♭ at step 1 and E♭ at step 4. Both are black keys to the LEFT of their white neighbours. RH saying numbers, then LH, then both together.

New Key
8min

B♭ Major Chords + Axis

Chord 1 = B♭–D–F. Chord 4 = E♭–G–B♭. Chord 5 = F–A–C (same as Chord 1 in F!). Chord 6m = G–B♭–D (Gm). Play Axis in B♭: B♭–F–Gm–E♭. Five times with LH bass notes.

B♭ Major
5min

Eyes-Closed B♭ Chords

Find Chord 1 (B♭–D–F) and Chord 4 (E♭–G–B♭) eyes closed. Both start on black keys — their tactile feel is very distinct from any chord you’ve played before.

Tuesday — Day 44Dominant 7th
5min

B♭ Scale + Chord Warmup

B♭ major scale both hands. Then 1–5–6m–4 in B♭ with LH bass notes.

Warmup
12min

Build Dominant 7th Chords

Add a flat 7th to any major chord. B♭7 = B♭–D–F–A♭. E♭7 = E♭–G–B♭–D♭. F7 = F–A–C–E♭. C7 = C–E–G–B♭. Play each and hear the urgency — the flat 7th creates an itch that demands to be scratched by the Chord 1 resolution.

Dominant 7th
8min

Dominant 7th Resolution Drill

B♭7 → E♭. F7 → B♭. C7 → F. Each dom7 pulls magnetically to its Chord 1. This V7→I resolution is the most powerful harmonic movement in Western music. 10 repetitions of each. Feel the release on every resolution.

Resolution
5min

Dom7 Ear Test

Play either a major chord or its dominant 7th version. Can you hear the added tension of the flat 7th? 10 rounds. The dom7 sounds like it has somewhere urgent to go — the major chord has already arrived.

Ear Training
Wednesday — Day 4512-Bar Blues
5min

Dynamics Warmup

Play Chord 1 in B♭: whisper soft, medium, loud, medium, soft. Five dynamic levels on one chord. Finger weight — not speed or force — controls volume.

Dynamics
15min

12-Bar Blues with All Dom7

The complete blues form. In B♭: B♭7(4 beats) B♭7 B♭7 B♭7 / E♭7 E♭7 B♭7 B♭7 / F7 E♭7 B♭7 F7. All three chords are dominant 7ths. This is the actual 12-bar blues used in every blues, rock, and jazz recording. The dom7 on every chord creates continuous, buzzing tension — the defining sound of the blues.

12-Bar Blues
10min

Blues in Three Keys

Play the full 12-bar blues with all dom7 chords in C, G, and B♭. Call a key, find the three 7th chords, play the 12-bar form. Each sounds slightly different but the pattern is identical. This is the number system at its most powerful.

Thursday — Day 46Boom-Chick + Rubato
5min

Finger Independence Drill

Both hands: play fingers 1-2-3-4-5 and back, slowly, one at a time. Every finger lifts fully, lands with equal weight. 30 seconds per hand.

Technique
12min

Boom-Chick in B♭

LH boom-chick on B♭: B♭ (root, beat 1) → B♭–D–F (chord, beat 2) → B♭ (root, beat 3) → chord (beat 4). Practice on B♭, E♭, F chords individually until automatic. Then apply to the full 1–4–5 progression. The boom-chick turns three chords into a complete-sounding accompaniment.

Boom-Chick
8min

Rubato in Practice

Play the Axis in B♭. Approach each chord change: slow down very slightly just before landing, then settle back to tempo after the chord. This is rubato — subtle forward-and-back breathing of the tempo. Louis Armstrong’s phrasing is full of it. Don’t overdo it; a little rubato goes a long way.

Rubato
5min

Seven-Key Eyes-Closed

Chord 1 in C, G, D, A, F, B♭ — and back. 3 rounds. Your two hands now know seven tonal centres.

Six Keys

Learn What a Wonderful World

What a Wonderful World — Louis Armstrong, B♭ Major. Core progression: B♭–Gm–E♭–F (1–6m–4–5). Apply boom-chick LH. The chord changes are very slow — often one chord every 2–4 bars. Play along with the original recording first at half volume. When the chords feel secure, add the melody in RH: it moves stepwise and is very singable, making it perfect for melody-by-ear. Use B♭ major pentatonic (B♭–C–D–F–G) as your melodic palette.

Real Song
10min

Dom7 Decoration

Take your What a Wonderful World chord progression and upgrade the F chord to F7 every time it appears. Feel how the F7 pulls back to B♭ with much more weight. Then try upgrading B♭ to B♭7 when it leads to E♭ — the dom7 on every chord leading to the next is the jazz sound.

Teacher's Notes

⚡

Dom7 Is the Engine

Every time you play V7→I, you’re using the most powerful harmonic motion in Western music. The blues works because every chord is dominant — continuous, buzzing tension that never fully resolves.

♭♭

B♭ Major Is Jazz Country

The home key of jazz trumpet, R&B saxophone, and gospel piano. Enormous repertoire opens up: What a Wonderful World, Fly Me to the Moon, Summertime, and hundreds more.

🌊

Rubato Is Not Rushing

Rubato means gently pulling back before a landing, then settling. It’s not speeding up or playing unevenly. Louis Armstrong’s genius was in timing — the note that arrives a fraction late creates unbearable beauty.

🎵

Stepwise Melody = Easier by Ear

What a Wonderful World moves almost entirely by steps (adjacent notes). This makes it one of the easiest beloved melodies to find by ear — you’re almost always one note up or down from where you are.

🎹

B♭ and E♭ Are Black-Key Starts

Both Chord 1 (B♭–D–F) and Chord 4 (E♭–G–B♭) start on black keys. Your fingers feel the difference immediately — these tactile anchors make B♭ major unmistakable by touch.
