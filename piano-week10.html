What this week is about

Modulation — changing key within a song — is the single most powerful expressive tool in music. Songs that modulate feel like they “lift off.” This week you learn three modulation methods and build them into Arrangement #3. You also add colour chords (add9, sus, slash) that make simple progressions sound produced and polished. Song: “Amazing Grace” — a perfect vehicle for all of this. You’ll play it in C major, then modulate up to D major for the final verse — a technique used in every gospel church performance of this hymn. The colour chords elevate a 3-chord song into something rich and personal.

🔀

Modulation

Three methods: (1) Direct lift: just start the new key. (2) Pivot chord: a chord belonging to both keys. (3) V7 announcement: play the V7 of the new key first. Each creates a different character of key change.

🎨

Colour Chords

add9: add the 9th above the root (Cadd9 = C–E–G–D). Sus chords: remove the 3rd. Slash chords: bass note not the root (C/E = C chord with E in the bass). These add texture without complication.

⚡

Real-Time <15 Seconds

A song starts and you have 15 seconds to find the key and start playing along. By this week this should feel achievable on most major-key pop and hymn-style songs.

📋

Arrangement #3

Complete arrangement of Amazing Grace with a modulation built in — C major for verses 1–2, D major for the final verse. Five sections, colour chords, full dynamic shape.

Three Modulation Methods

1) Direct lift: end on Chord 5 of the old key, then simply start the new key on its Chord 1. Simple and dramatic. 2) Pivot chord: find a chord that belongs to both keys — e.g. G major is Chord 5 in C AND Chord 4 in D. 3) V7 announcement: play the V7 of the new key first (A7 announces D major). Practice all three modulating from C to D.

Modulation
8min

Amazing Grace Modulation

Learn the C→D modulation for Amazing Grace. Play verse 1 in C (C–F–G), then at the final verse lift directly to D major (D–G–A). The whole-step lift gives the final verse an uplifting, revelatory feeling — exactly why gospel pianists use it. Play the transition 10 times until it feels inevitable.

Application
5min

Modulation Ear Test

Listen to 3 songs you know: can you hear a key change anywhere? Familiar modulations to listen for: key lifts at final choruses, half-step or whole-step rises. Even identifying one modulation by ear this week is a win.

Build Colour Chords

add9: Cadd9 = C–E–G–D. Open, modern sound. Gadd9 = G–B–D–A. Slash chord: C/E = C major with E in the bass (LH plays E, RH plays C–E–G). G/B = G major with B in the bass. Creates a smooth descending bass line: C–G/B–Am. Sus4 review: Csus4–C before each chord. These three techniques turn plain chords into something produced and rich.

Colour Chords
10min

Colour Chords on Amazing Grace

Take the Amazing Grace progression (C–F–G in 3/4) and decorate it: use Cadd9 on the opening Chord 1, G/B as a smooth passing chord between C and Am, Fsus4–F before Chord 4 arrives. Same 3-chord hymn — completely different character.

Application
3min

add9 vs Plain Major

Play C, then Cadd9. That single added D note opens the chord up — less dense, more spacious. 10 rounds: play one, identify which. The add9 has a slightly less “complete” feeling — more searching, more modern.

Waltz Bass in B♭ and C

3/4 time: root on beat 1, chord on beats 2 and 3. Practice in C (C–F–G) and D (D–G–A). Amazing Grace is in 3/4 — waltz bass is the natural LH pattern. Get it automatic before building the full arrangement.

Waltz Bass
20min

Build Amazing Grace — Complete Arrangement

Intro: Cmaj7 held 4 beats, arpeggiated LH, pp. Verse 1: C–F–G waltz bass, RH melody softly. Verse 2: C–F–G with colour chord decoration (Cadd9, G/B, Fsus4–F), slightly louder. Build to modulation: end of verse 2 on G7 (V7 of C — also V7 announcing D’s IV if you treat it as pivot). Final verse: lift to D major (D–G–A), forte dynamics, full tone. Outro: Dmaj7, slow, fade.

Teacher's Notes

🔀

Modulation Creates a Lift

A key change is a promise: the music is about to intensify. When you land in the new key on a strong beat with strong dynamics, the listener feels it physically. The C→D lift in Amazing Grace should feel like the sun coming out.

🎨

Colour Chords Are Subtle

add9, sus, slash — these work because they’re subtle. One colour chord per phrase is plenty. Overuse them and the colour becomes noise. Amazing Grace with just a Cadd9 on the opening chord already sounds transformed.

⚡

15 Seconds Is a Muscle

Getting into a song in 15 seconds requires the ear-to-hand pathway to be nearly automatic. The only way to build it is reps. 10 songs per session. Hymns and folk songs are especially good for this because their harmonies are clear.

🎵

Amazing Grace Is Deceptively Deep

Three chords, universally known, 3/4 time — sounds simple. But with waltz bass, colour chords, dynamic shaping, and a modulation, it becomes a complete and genuinely moving arrangement. Simplicity is not a limitation here.

🔢

Slash Chords Are Just Bass Notes

G/B means: G major chord, but put B in the bass. LH plays B, RH plays G major. Once you see it that way, it’s entirely straightforward — and the descending bass effect sounds professional immediately.

📅

Sustain pedal, embellishments, phrasing arcs, and the final polish of all three arrangements. Then Week 12: the final recording session. Three weeks from the finish line.
