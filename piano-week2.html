Core Theory

🎵 The 6 Minor Chord — Major vs Minor Quality

One Note Changes Everything

A major chord sounds bright and open. A minor chord sounds darker and more emotional. The only difference is the middle note — the third. Lower the third by one half-step (one key to the left) and the chord turns minor. In C major, the 6th degree is A, so Chord 6m = A–C–E (A minor).

1C–E–GMajor
2mD–F–Aminor
3mE–G–Bminor
4F–A–CMajor
5G–B–DMajor
6mA–C–Eminor
7°B–D–Fdim

This quality pattern (Maj–min–min–Maj–Maj–min–dim) is the same in every major key forever. Memorise it once; it never changes.

The 1–5–6m–4 “Axis” Progression

The most common progression in Western pop. In C: C → G → Am → F. The 6m creates an emotional wave — bright (1) → tense (5) → melancholic (6m) → lifting (4). Hundreds of hit songs use exactly this loop.

1 (C)→5 (G)→6m (Am)→4 (F)

This Week’s Song — Play Along

🎵 Let It Be — The Beatles

Your First Real Song

This week you will play along with an actual recording. “Let It Be” by The Beatles uses the exact 1–5–6m–4 Axis progression in C major: C–G–Am–F. The chord changes are slow (roughly every 2 beats in the verse), and the key is C major — the key you already know.

Goal: put the recording on, and play the chord skeleton (LH bass note on beat 1 + RH chord on beats 2–3–4). You do not need the melody yet — the chords are the song.

🎵 Let It Be — The Beatles
Key: C Major  ·  Progression: C – G – Am – F (1–5–6m–4)  ·  Chord rhythm: ~2 beats per chord in verse  ·  Why it works: slow changes, well-known melody, crystal-clear Axis structure

Daily Practice Plans

Monday — Day 8New Chord
5min

Warmup: Week 1 Review

Play C major scale RH, then LH. Then play Chord 1→4→5→1 three times. Eyes closed on the last round. This becomes your permanent 5-minute opener every week.

Review
10min

Build the 6 Minor (Am)

The 6th degree of C major is A. Chord 6m = A–C–E. RH: finger 1 on A, 3 on C, 5 on E. Play it 10 times alone. Then alternate: Chord 1 (C–E–G) → Chord 6m (A–C–E). Hear the shift from bright to dark.

New Chord
10min

1–5–6m–4 Progression

RH only: C(4 beats) → G → Am → F. Repeat 5×. Say the numbers aloud: “one… five… six minor… four.” Feel the emotional arc — especially how Am lands after G. This is the sound of “Let It Be.”

Axis Progression
5min

Major or Minor Ear Game

Play either Chord 1 (C major) or Chord 6m (Am) without looking. Before checking — bright or dark? 10 rounds. Your ear is learning to distinguish chord quality.

Ear Training
Tuesday — Day 9Coordination
5min

Finger 4–5 Isolation

Place both hands flat on a table. Lift ONLY finger 4, then ONLY finger 5, alternating. 30 seconds each hand. Fingers 4 and 5 are weakest — daily targeted work is the only fix.

Dexterity
10min

LH Bass + RH Chord Split

LH plays single bass notes on beat 1: C for chord 1, G for chord 5, A for chord 6m, F for chord 4. RH plays the chord on beats 2–3–4. Go slowly through 1–5–6m–4. This is real piano accompaniment.

Coordination
10min

Voice Leading: C Major → Am

C major (C–E–G) and Am (A–C–E) share two notes. Instead of jumping, keep C and E in place — only move G down to A. Practice C→Am→F→G smoothly. Minimal movement = professional sound.

Voice Leading
5min

Arpeggio Introduction

Instead of pressing all chord notes at once, play them one at a time: C, then E, then G. Go through 1–5–6m–4 as arpeggios. Sounds immediately musical at any tempo.

Arpeggios
Wednesday — Day 10Ear Training
5min

Stretch + Keyboard Mapping

Gently pull each finger back. Roll wrists. Then eyes closed: find C, F, G, A by feel without looking. The spatial map of your keyboard is building in your hands.

Flexibility
10min

Key Detection: First Attempt

Search YouTube for “C major backing track.” Listen 30 seconds, then find the 1 chord by ear. Then layer in 5, 6m, 4. Imperfect is correct — the process of trying IS the training.

Key Detection
10min

Progression Rotation Drill

Play each loop twice through in C: 1–5–6m–4 (Axis), 1–4–5 (blues loop), 1–6m–4–5 (doo-wop). Same chords — completely different emotional feel each time.

Patterns
5min

Audiation Exercise

Play Chord 1. Then — without playing — hum what you think Chord 4 sounds like. Then play Chord 4 to check. Do the same for 6m and 5. Hearing before playing is the start of musical thought.

Ear Training
Thursday — Day 11Rhythm
5min

Polyrhythm Tap

LH taps a steady beat (1–2–3–4) on your knee. RH taps on beats 2 and 4 only (the “backbeat”). This independence is identical to what you’ll do on keys: one hand steady, one syncopated.

Independence
12min

LH Bass + RH Arpeggio

LH plays root note on beat 1. RH arpeggios the chord on beats 2, 3, 4. Through 1–5–6m–4 at 60 BPM. Sounds like real piano accompaniment immediately.

Rhythm
8min

Play Along: Let It Be

Put on “Let It Be” by The Beatles. Play C–G–Am–F with LH bass notes + RH chords. Don’t worry about matching every detail — stay in the loop and keep going when you miss a change.

Real Song
5min

Eyes-Closed: 4-Chord Hunt

Eyes closed. Find chord 1, 4, 5, and 6m one at a time. 10 rounds. 8/10 correct = you own these chords by feel.

Board Memory
Friday — Day 12Song Work
5min

Spider Stretch

Place finger 1 on C, 2 on D. Stretch finger 3 to E while 1–2 stay down. Add finger 4 to F. Hold 3 seconds. Builds flexibility for wider chord positions.

Flexibility

Deepen: Let It Be

Work specifically on the Am (6m) chord entry — the emotional centre of the song. Play the loop C–G–Am–F with arpeggios in RH. Try to hear the moment the Am lands and let that feeling drive your playing. That landing IS music.

Deep Song Work
10min

Free Play: Emotional Exploration

Play the Axis slowly. Make it sound “sad” by dwelling on Am longer. Then “hopeful” by spending more time on Chord 1. The emotional arc changes purely from timing. This is your first lesson in musical expression.

Interval Ear Training

Play Middle C then E — major 3rd: the bright interval of every major chord. Play C then E♭ — minor 3rd: the darker sound of every minor chord. Hum both. Recognising these intervals is how you identify chord quality by ear.

Pick 5 songs from your playlist. For each: 1) listen 30 seconds, 2) decide major or minor, 3) hum the gravity note, 4) find it on piano, 5) test chords 1, 4, 5, 6m. Write down your findings — wrong guesses included.

Key Detection
10min

Listening Assignment: Amazing Grace

Put on “Amazing Grace.” Listen only to chord changes — tap your finger every time the harmony shifts. Can you hear that it uses chords 1, 4, and 5? This timeless hymn is a perfect ear training vehicle.

Write 3 songs you heard this week. For each: major or minor? Any chords you could name? The habit of conscious listening accelerates ear development faster than any exercise.

Teacher's Notes

🎭

The 6m Is Emotional Power

The 6 minor is not simply “sad.” It creates depth, longing, and the feeling of something beautiful and slightly out of reach. Use it deliberately and it becomes craft.

🔄

Voice Leading Matters

C major (C–E–G) and Am (A–C–E) share two notes. Keep shared notes still and only move what must move. This is how pros make chord changes sound seamless.

👂

Ear Training Happens Everywhere

While commuting, cooking — tap chord changes. Every time the harmony shifts, tap. These micro-sessions add up to hours of ear training weekly.

⏱️

Wrong Speed = Wrong Learning

If transitions are clunky, cut tempo in half. The brain encodes pattern quality, not speed. Speed arrives automatically once the pattern is locked.

🎯

Chords Before Melody

Playing 4 chords in time with Let It Be is the correct goal this week. Melody comes in Weeks 5–8 once chord transitions are automatic.

🧠

Name What You Hear

Every time a chord feels emotionally “right” in a song, try to name it. “That sounded tense — probably Chord 5.” Connecting emotion to number builds musical vocabulary fast.
