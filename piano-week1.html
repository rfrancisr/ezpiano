What this week is about

Week 1 is entirely about mental rewiring. Instead of note names (A B C D E F G) or sheet music, you will learn to think in numbers 1 through 7. This is the Nashville Number System — the same tool session musicians use to play any song in any key without reading a single note. You'll also begin waking up your hands with finger independence exercises and train your ear to hear whether a chord feels "settled" or "tense."

🔢

Number System

Every note in a key gets a number 1–7. Chords built on those notes inherit the same number. Key changes? Just shift the numbers.

🖐️

Hand Technique

Curved fingers, relaxed wrist, no tension. Independent finger movement before speed. We build the machine first.

👂

Ear Training

The "at home" feeling of 1 (tonic). The "wanting to resolve" tension of 5. You'll start hearing music differently within days.

🧠

Board Memory

Every session closes your eyes and finds notes by feel. No looking — your hands must eventually know where they live.

Core Theory for Week 1

⚙️  The Nashville Number System — Your New Language

Why Numbers Beat Note Names

When you learn a song as "C – F – G – C" you can only play it in C major. When you learn it as "1 – 4 – 5 – 1" you can instantly play it in ANY key just by starting on a different note. Every professional musician who plays by ear uses this system.

This week we anchor everything in C Major because all the notes are on the white keys — your training wheels key. Once the concept is solid in C, moving to other keys is trivial.

1 C Home
2 D Supertonic
3 E Mediant
4 F Sub-Dom
5 G Dominant
6 A Relative m
7 B Leading

The Major Scale Formula — this is how every major key is built from any starting note:

Root → Whole → Whole → Half → Whole → Whole → Whole → Half

The 3 Chords That Unlock 90% of Pop Songs

A chord is built by stacking every other number: 1 + 3 + 5. So chord 1 in C major = C + E + G. This week you'll learn only three chords:

Chord 1 — C E G  (Home) Chord 4 — F A C  (Departure) Chord 5 — G B D  (Tension)

Listen for the emotional feeling: 1 sounds settled, 4 sounds like movement, 5 sounds like it needs to come back to 1. This 1–4–5 relationship is the DNA of blues, country, folk, and pop.

02

Daily Practice Plans

Monday — Day 1 Theory + Setup
5min

Meet Your Keyboard

Find every C on the keyboard (black key landmark: C is always to the LEFT of the 2-black-key group). Name them from left to right. Press each one and listen — same note, different octaves.

Board Memory
10min

Play the C Major Scale — Right Hand

Start on Middle C. Finger 1 (thumb) = C, 2 = D, 3 = E, then tuck thumb under to 4=F, 5=G. Back down. Go slow — each note is one number. Say the numbers OUT LOUD: "one, two, three, four, five, six, seven, one."

Technique + Numbers
10min

Learn Chord 1 (C Major)

Place fingers 1-3-5 of your right hand on C-E-G. Press together — that's your 1 chord. Practice pressing, lifting, pressing. Then try with LEFT hand: fingers 5-3-1 on C-E-G. Hold for 4 counts, release, repeat.

Chord Shape
5min

Eyes-Closed Recall

Close your eyes. Can you find Middle C? Find the 1 chord? Open eyes to check. Repeat until you land on it 3 times in a row with eyes closed.

Board Memory
Tuesday — Day 2 Technique Focus
5min

Finger Independence Warmup

Place both hands flat on a table. Lift ONE finger at a time while keeping the others down. Start with finger 1 (thumb), then 2, 3, 4, 5. Go slowly. If finger 4 refuses to lift alone — that's normal! Work it daily.

Dexterity
10min

Scale Review + Left Hand Scale

Right hand scale up and down 3x saying numbers aloud. Then LEFT hand: finger 5 on C, 4=D, 3=E, 2=F, tuck 1 (thumb) for G, 2=A, 3=B, 4=C. Slow is right. Match the sound of each note to its number.

Numbers + Technique
10min

Learn Chord 5 (G Major)

G is scale degree 5. Chord 5 = G + B + D. Right hand: find G above Middle C, place 1-3-5 on G-B-D. Play Chord 1, then Chord 5. Hear how Chord 5 feels "unfinished" — it wants to go back to Chord 1. This tension is the heart of all music.

Ear Training + Chords
5min

1 → 5 → 1 Loop

Play: Chord 1 (4 beats) → Chord 5 (4 beats) → Chord 1 (4 beats). Repeat 5 times. Hum along. Feel the resolution when you land back on 1.

Ear Training
Wednesday — Day 3 Ear Training
5min

Table Taps + Stretches

Before touching keys: stretch fingers back gently (never force). Tap each finger independently on a table, both hands simultaneously. 30 seconds each hand. Shake out wrists loosely.

Flexibility
10min

Chord 4 (F Major)

F is scale degree 4. Chord 4 = F + A + C. Right hand: finger 1 on F, 3 on A, 5 on C. Now you have all three chords. Play them in order: 1 → 4 → 5 → 1. Say "one… four… five… one" as you play. Notice how each has a different emotional flavor.

Chords + Numbers
10min

1-4-5 Rotation Drill

Set a slow metronome (60 BPM or count "1-2-3-4" steadily). Play: 1 chord for 4 beats, 4 chord for 4 beats, 5 chord for 4 beats, back to 1 for 4 beats. Repeat 5 rounds. This 12-bar blues pattern is in hundreds of songs.

Chord Transitions
5min

Ear Game: 1 or 5?

Play either the 1 chord or the 5 chord with your eyes closed. Before looking — try to identify: does it feel "home" (1) or "tense/unresolved" (5)? Play 5 rounds. This is your first real ear training exercise.

Ear Training
Thursday — Day 4 Consolidation
5min

Finger Crawl Exercise

Right hand on C. Play: 1-2-3-4-5-4-3-2-1 (C D E F G F E D C) slowly. Keep fingers curved, wrist still. Each note should ring clearly — no buzzing from adjacent keys. Try on left hand too.

Dexterity + Clarity
10min

Hands Together: Scale

Play the C major scale with BOTH hands together, moving in parallel. Left hand starts one octave below right hand. Go VERY slow. The goal isn't speed — it's making both hands cooperate. This builds hand independence.

Coordination
10min

"Twist" Pattern — Real Song Rhythm

Left hand plays the root note of each chord (just the single note C, F, or G). Right hand plays the chord. Pattern: Left bass note on beat 1, right chord on beat 2, left bass on beat 3, right chord on beat 4. This is how pianists comp in pop and soul.

Rhythm + Independence
5min

Sing the Scale

Play each note of C major and sing "Do Re Mi Fa Sol La Ti Do" — or just hum. Match your voice to the piano pitch. Your ear is the most important instrument you own. This connects pitch to muscle memory.

Ear Training
Friday — Day 5 Technique Deep Dive
5min

Hanon-Style Crawler

Play this pattern starting on C: 1-2-3-4, 2-3-4-5, 3-4-5-6, 4-5-6-7, 5-6-7-8 (each number = the next white key). Climb up the keyboard and back down. One hand at a time. This builds sequential strength in all fingers.

Strength + Dexterity
10min

Chord Inversions Preview

You know Chord 1 as C-E-G (root position). Now try: E-G-C (first inversion) and G-C-E (second inversion). These are the SAME chord, rearranged. Play all three in sequence. Notice how they share notes with neighboring chords — this is why voice leading between 1, 4, 5 is so smooth.

Theory + Board Memory
10min

Free Play: I-IV-V Any Order

Put on a simple backing drum loop (YouTube: "60 BPM drum loop"). Play chords 1, 4, and 5 in any order that feels good. Don't think too hard. Experiment. Let your ear guide when to change chords. This is your first musical improvisation!

Creative Play
5min

Eyes-Closed Chord Hunt

Eyes closed. Someone (or your phone) says a number: 1, 4, or 5. You find and play the chord. 10 rounds. Track how many you get right — your score will improve each week.

Board Memory
Saturday — Day 6 Ear + Song Work
5min

Hum & Find

Hum any note that comes naturally. Find it on the piano. That note is your "1" right now. Try to play the scale starting there. No pressure to get it right — just explore. This is how your ear will eventually identify keys in real songs.

Ear + Board Memory
15min

First Real Song: 12-Bar Blues

The 12-bar blues is the most important chord progression in Western music. Pattern in C: 1(4) 1(4) 1(4) 1(4) / 4(4) 4(4) 1(4) 1(4) / 5(4) 4(4) 1(4) 5(4). Numbers in () = beats. Play with left hand root notes + right hand chords. This is the skeleton of hundreds of hit songs.

Real Music
10min

Listen + Tap: Active Ear Training

Play a song you love on Spotify. Just tap your foot to the beat and try to COUNT — feel where beat 1 is. Most pop songs are in 4/4 (4 beats per measure). Can you feel the "landing" on beat 1? This is the beginning of hearing structure in music.

Ear Training
Sunday — Day 7 Review + Reflect
10min

Full Week Review

Play through everything you learned: C major scale (both hands), Chord 1, Chord 4, Chord 5. Then play the 1-4-5-1 progression. No rushing — just smooth and confident. If something feels shaky, spend extra time there next week.

Consolidation
10min

Eyes-Closed Full Test

Eyes closed. From memory, play: scale up and down, then Chord 1, then Chord 4, then Chord 5, then Chord 1 again. Open eyes to check. This builds the spatial map of your keyboard in your brain — essential for playing without looking.

Board Memory
10min

Journal + Listen Assignment

Write 3 things: 1) What felt easy this week. 2) What felt hard. 3) One song you want to eventually learn. Then listen to that song and just feel the pulse — try to count measures. You're already hearing music differently.

Reflection + Ear
03

Teacher's Notes for Week 1

🐢

Slow Is Fast

Playing slowly and correctly teaches your muscles the right patterns. Speed comes automatically once those patterns are locked in. Never practice a mistake at speed.

🔢

Always Say the Numbers

When you play the scale, say "1 2 3 4 5 6 7 1" out loud. When you play chords, say "one chord… four chord… five chord." Verbal reinforcement burns the number system into long-term memory.

😤

Tension = Stop

If your wrist, forearm, or shoulder feels tight — stop. Shake out your hands. Piano should feel effortless. Tension causes injuries and slows learning.

👁️

Practice Not Looking

Every session, spend at least 2 minutes with eyes closed. Concert pianists memorize the entire geography of the keyboard. This starts on Day 1, not later.

🎵

Listen Actively Daily

While commuting, cooking, or exercising — listen to music and try to tap the beat. Count to 4 repeatedly. Identify when the chord might change. Your ear trains outside of practice time.

🎯

One Goal Per Session

Don't try to fix everything at once. Each day has a primary focus. If chord transitions are rough, that's next week's problem. Do what today's plan says.
