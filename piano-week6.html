Core Theory

♭ F Major — Your First Flat Key

The Other Direction on the Circle

Every key so far went right (sharp). F Major goes left: it adds B♭ at step 4. W-W-H-W-W-W-H from F naturally lands on B♭. The black key goes to the left of B instead of the right.

Key chords in F: Chord 1 = F–A–C  ·  Chord 4 = B♭–D–F (gospel!)  ·  Chord 5 = C–E–G (= Chord 1 in C!)  ·  Chord 6m = D–F–A (Dm)

That B♭–D–F chord is the sound of gospel and soul — Stevie Wonder, gospel choirs, R&B everywhere. Play F–C–Dm–B♭ (Axis in F) five times and feel the character of flat keys: warm, rounded, soulful.

👁 Three Left-Hand Patterns — Same Chords, Three Genres

The LH Defines the Style

The same chord progression played with three different LH patterns sounds like three completely different genres.

Boom-chick:Root(1) Chord(2) Root(3) Chord(4) — sounds country/pop
Waltz:Root(1) Chord(2) Chord(3) — 3/4 time, sounds like a waltz
Arpeggiated:Root(1) 5th(2) Chord(3) 5th(4) — sounds like a ballad

This week you drill all three on the Axis in C and F. Once your LH pattern is automatic, your RH can play a melody or improvise freely — which is exactly what you’ll do next week.

📰 Mapping a Complete Song

From Fragments to a Full Lead Sheet

Until now you’ve guessed a few chords. This week you map a complete song — every section — written in numbers. Good first candidates: “Knockin’ on Heaven’s Door” (only 3 chords, very slow), “No Woman No Cry” (slow, clear C major), or “Amazing Grace” (3 chords, 3/4 time).

1 Find key→2 Map verse→3 Map chorus→4 Write in numbers

The result is your first number lead sheet: a complete chord map you can play in any key instantly. Every song you map from now on adds to your permanent library.

“Knockin’ on Heaven’s Door” by Bob Dylan uses only three chords in G Major: G–D–Am and G–D–C. The chord changes are very slow and clear — ideal for your first complete song map. Boom-Chick Pattern

LH on C major: C (finger 5) on beat 1, then C–E–G chord on beat 2, C again beat 3, chord beat 4. Practice on C, F, G chords until automatic. Then 1–5–6m–4 in C with boom-chick throughout.

Dynamic Progression

Arpeggiated LH Pattern

For each Axis chord in C: LH plays root(C) on beat 1, the 5th(G) on beat 2, full chord on beat 3, 5th(G) again on beat 4. This root–5th–chord–5th pattern is used in every ballad and creates a gentle rolling feel.

03

Teacher's Notes

🏆

You’ve Earned This

Six weeks ago you knew nothing. Now you know 5 keys, 6 chord types, 8+ progressions, 3 LH patterns, melody by ear, key detection. This is real musical knowledge.

♭

Flats Are Not Harder

The B♭ may feel awkward because every previous black key went right. Your hand adjusts within 2–3 days. Flat keys are the home of jazz, gospel, and soul.

🗺️

Song Mapping Changes Everything

Once you can write a progression in numbers, you can play it in any key instantly. Songs stop being mysteries and become examples of patterns you already own.

🎭

Dynamics = Emotion

Technical accuracy gets you 60% of the way to sounding musical. The remaining 40% is dynamics. Simple music played expressively will always sound better than complex music played flatly.

🔄

LH Patterns Are Styles

Boom-chick is country/pop. Waltz is a waltz. Arpeggiated is ballad. Knowing when to deploy each one is as important as knowing how to play them.

📅

The Second Half Plan

Weeks 7–9: B♭ Major, 7th chords (the jazz/R&B upgrade), and full song arrangements. Weeks 10–12: modulation, advanced ear, and your final recital. The foundation is complete — now we build the house.
